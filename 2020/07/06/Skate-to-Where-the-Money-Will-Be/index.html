Skate to Where the Money Will Be

2020-07-06

How Value Chains Evolve

  • Stage 1: A Tight Fit.

    • Products are not good enough to meet customer demands/needs.

    • Large established, vertically integrated companies dominate.

      When the product isn’t good enough, in other words, being an integrated company is critical to success.

  • Stage 2: Going to Pieces.

    • Product performance overshhots mainstream customers needs.
    • Modular and flexible, customized and cheaper products dominate.

  • Fuzzy Links

    When a company is considering whether it’s feasible to procure a subsystem from a supplier or partner rather than make it in-house. Three conditions must be met:

    • Specifiability: Know what attributes are to procure and what are not.
    • Verifiability: Measure to verify that received what they need.
    • Predictability: Understand how the subsystem will perform with the other pieces to be used with predictable effect.

Where the money went in the PC industry

For most of this period, profits lodeged with the products that were the ones not yet good enough for what their immediate customers needed.

Where Companies Go Wrong

Once an industry starts to fragment, a very predictable thing happens to companies that design and assemble modular products.

  1. They face investor pressure to improve their return on assets but find that because they can’t differentiate their products or make them at lower cost than competitors, they can’t improve the numerator of their ROA ratio.
  2. They shrink the denominator; they sell off asset-intensive units that design and manufacture components to companies that see in those same operations the opportunity to create subsystems whose architectures are progresively more interdependent – thus improving the numerator of their ROA ratio.

But with perfect predictability, this pressure from Wall Street to boost ROA forces companies to skate away from the place where the money will be made in the future.

Where Will the Money Be in the Auto Industry?

The most reliable cars usually go out of style long before they wear out.

IBS’s recent forays into opportunistic decouping, ignored the siren song of investment bankers and found a way not to shet those asset and scale-intensive business where the numerator of the ROA ratio will likely be more attractive in the future. This will be especially true if shifts in customer demand mandate some sort of reintegration in the future.

Summary

The power to capture attractive profits will shift in the value chain to those activities where the immediate customer is not yet satisfied with the functionality of available products. It’s in these stages that complex, interdependent integration occurs. The power will shift away from activities where the immediate customer is more than satisfied because its there that standard, modular integration occurs. In most markets, this power shift occurs tier by tier in a way that is quite predictable.

Don’t wonder whether the power to earn attractive profits will shift, but when.
